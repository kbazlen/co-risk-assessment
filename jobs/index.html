Methodology

How climate exposure is calculated and how job sector risk is determined.

1. Climate data & raster overlay

Each layer is a GeoTIFF raster file at either ~10km resolution (~0.09° cells, on the coarse grid) or ~3 km resolution (~0.03° cells, on the fine grid). These layers, which can be viewed by enabling the raster overlay, contain data interpolated from the original climate model analysis fields to WGS84 coordinates which are evenly-spaced across the state of Colorado for clarity of presentation. This process introduces some smoothing, but preserves overall patterns in the data.

The county choropleth (shown without the overlay) uses the spatial mean of all data points within each county boundary—a zonal statistics operation run in Python using rasterio and geopandas—from the original, non-interpolated climate model analysis fields. Files named minus_REF show the change from a historical baseline (~1980–2010), not an absolute value.

3. Exposure score formula

exposure_score = county_jobs × outdoor_weight × hazard_change_value

Each sector has an outdoor exposure weight — the estimated fraction of workers performing work outdoors or in conditions directly affected by the hazard. The bar charts show exposed workers (jobs × weight). The score is a relative index for ranking, not a forecast of job losses.

4. Sector weights & what "low risk" means

A sector is greyed out when the selected hazard type has no meaningful direct pathway to affect that sector's workers. This does not mean zero economic impact — supply chain, infrastructure, and consumer-spending effects are real but not captured here. This tool focuses on direct worker exposure only.

Weights are derived primarily from the BLS Occupational Requirements Survey (ORS), which measures the fraction of workers in each occupation group who perform critical tasks outdoors, unprotected from the elements. Where ORS does not cover a sector (notably agriculture), weights are inferred from O*NET work context ratings and OSHA enforcement data. Confidence indicators:

✓ direct ORS   BLS ORS publishes an occupation group figure that directly covers this sector.   ~ inferred   Expert estimate derived from adjacent ORS groups or other federal data.   ⚠ data gap   ORS excludes this sector by design; weight is based on O*NET and OSHA data only.

5. Limitations

Outdoor weights are sector-level averages. Employment from BLS QCEW may undercount informal and seasonal workers. The exposure score is a relative index for comparison — not a forecast of job losses or economic damage. Treat sector weights as starting-point estimates to be refined with local expertise.
